Project Hail Mary: Live Algorithmic Trading System
Jun 2026 to Present
Python · Zerodha Kite Connect API · DigitalOcean · C++ · JIRA

Project Hail Mary is an intraday algorithmic trading system that trades BANKBEES, a Bank Nifty ETF, using an Opening Range Breakout (ORB) strategy layered with a few extra filters to screen out weak setups. Each day it watches the market's opening range, waits for a confirmed breakout, and manages the trade automatically from entry through exit.

I started this because I wanted to be able to stand independently on my own feet. The idea itself sounds almost too simple: spend five minutes every morning, then leave it alone, and it makes a real net return over the year. Of course, that simplicity is earned. Getting to that point took a huge amount of setup, testing, and fixing before it actually ran the way it was supposed to.

One of the earliest bugs was almost embarrassingly simple in hindsight: I hadn't converted the system's clock from UTC to IST, so orders were firing at the wrong time entirely, and the whole module was quietly broken for a while before I caught it. Small mistake, big consequence, and a good early lesson in how much infrastructure "invisible" details actually need.

The system now runs live with real money through the Kite Connect API. If it crashes, it can safely pick back up where it left off, it won't accidentally run two copies of itself at once, and it checks in constantly to make sure everything's working. It also moves the stop-loss forward in four steps as a trade goes well, so the maximum possible loss keeps shrinking the further the trade moves in my favor. Backtested over two years of data, it shows a 35.7% net annual return with a 1.48 profit factor.

Working Micro-Spectrometer Model
Oct 2025 to Mar 2026
Optics · CAD (Solid Edge) · 3D Printing

A micro-spectrometer is essentially a microscope and a spectrometer combined into one device. A microscope magnifies a sample so fine physical detail becomes visible; a spectrometer analyzes how a sample interacts with light to reveal information about its composition. Put together, this device lets you see a sample up close and understand what it's made of at the same time.

The hardest part of this project was integrating real, physical parts into a 3D-printed setup. We used a rack-and-pinion mechanism salvaged from my grandfather's LED factory, since he manufactures LEDs and had a stock of old parts lying around that turned out to be exactly what we needed, along with a top light for the sample. Getting those real components to fit cleanly into 3D-printed housing meant measuring everything carefully with vernier calipers, modeling it precisely in Solid Edge, and iterating until it actually printed and fit correctly.

The harder challenge came when we converted the setup from a microscope into a spectrometer. That meant aligning multiple optical elements together at once: a diffraction grating, an endoscope camera, a blade slit set at a 0.1mm gap, and a microscope glass slide, extremely thin, all needing to sit at a precise 45° angle relative to each other. Getting that alignment right, physically, was the most technically demanding part of the whole build.

M-IP Slag Viscosity Simulation
May 2026 to Present
Python · with Prof. Ashok Kamaraj, IIT Madras

Slag is the leftover byproduct when metal is extracted from ore at high temperature, mostly waste minerals and impurities. How easily it flows, its viscosity, matters a lot in a furnace: slag that's too thick can clog the process and slow everything down.

I got interested in this through my Ironmaking and Steelmaking classes. The subject itself didn't grab me at first, but the sheer scale of it did. A single blast furnace produces an enormous amount of metal, and the plant sizes involved are hard to picture until you actually see the numbers. That scale made me want to try modeling a small piece of that world computationally, which is how this project started.

The work reproduces a published method for measuring high-temperature slag viscosity in Python, validated against the paper's own reported results across seven ash samples and four incline angles. I then extended it to match a real lab furnace setup, adding transient heat transfer, friction, and temperature-dependent viscosity, solved together as a coupled system over a 40-minute simulated furnace cycle per sample.
